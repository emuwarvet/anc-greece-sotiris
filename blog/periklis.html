Pericles (495-429 BCE)

Athenian statesman and leader of the Golden Age

"What you leave behind is not what is engraved in stone monuments, but what is woven into the lives of others."

Biography

Pericles was a prominent and influential Greek statesman, orator, and general of Athens during its Golden Age, specifically the time between the Persian and Peloponnesian Wars. He was descended, through his mother, from the powerful and historically influential Alcmaeonid family.

Pericles had such a profound influence on Athenian society that Thucydides, a contemporary historian, acclaimed him as "the first citizen of Athens." His period of leadership is often called the "Age of Pericles."

Achievements

  • Oversaw the construction of the Parthenon and other monuments
  • Promoted arts, literature, and philosophy in Athens
  • Strengthened Athenian democracy and citizenship
  • Led Athens during its height as a naval empire
  • Delivered the famous Funeral Oration

Political Philosophy

Pericles believed in the power of democracy and the importance of civic participation. He promoted the idea that Athens should be a model for other cities, emphasizing the values of freedom, culture, and intellectual achievement.

His vision of Athens as an "education to Greece" reflected his belief that the city should lead by example, showing the world the benefits of democratic governance and cultural excellence.

The Funeral Oration

Pericles' most famous speech, delivered during the first year of the Peloponnesian War, praised the unique qualities of Athens and its democratic system. The speech, recorded by Thucydides, remains one of the greatest examples of political oratory and continues to influence democratic thought today.

Legacy

Pericles' leadership transformed Athens into the intellectual and cultural center of the ancient world. The buildings and institutions he established influenced Western civilization for millennia, and his ideas about democracy continue to resonate today.

Socrates (470-399 BCE)

The father of Western philosophy and the Socratic method

"The unexamined life is not worth living."

Biography

Socrates was a classical Greek philosopher credited as one of the founders of Western philosophy. He is an enigmatic figure known chiefly through the accounts of classical writers, especially the writings of his students Plato and Xenophon.

Born in Athens, Socrates spent most of his life there, engaging fellow citizens in philosophical discussions. He developed the Socratic method of inquiry, which seeks to get to the truth by asking a series of questions until logical contradictions expose the ignorance of the respondent.

Key Teachings

  • The Socratic Method - learning through questioning
  • "Know thyself" - the importance of self-knowledge
  • Virtue is knowledge - no one does wrong willingly
  • The pursuit of wisdom over material possessions

Legacy

Socrates' influence on philosophy and thought is immeasurable. Though he wrote nothing himself, his ideas live on through the works of Plato and continue to shape philosophical inquiry today. His commitment to truth and virtue, even unto death, exemplifies the philosophical life.
